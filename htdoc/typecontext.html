T26. Left and Right Context in Types

In some places, it is important whether a certain type occurs on the left-hand or right-hand side of -> in a type. The reason is that functions behave differently in their domains from in their codomains. When given a parameter (the domain), a function must perform analysis (breaking the parameter down into its parts). When constructing its result (the codomain) a function must perform synthesis (constructing something from its parts).

Given a type, it is possible to label each part according to context. You start in a right context (like the right side of ->). As you move into structured types, you normally maintain the current context. When you enter the left-hand side of ->, however, you switch to the opposite context.

For example, in type A -> (B -> (C,D)), A and B are in left context, but C and D are in right context.

In (A -> B) -> C, A and C are in right context, but B is in left context. (You begin in right context, but switch to left when labeling (A -> B). You do not switch for labeling B, so it is in left context. Since A is on the left-hand side of ->, you switch again, to right context.)

One place where context is important is in the definition of transparent families. A variable is not allowed to occur in a left context in the representation type of a transparent family. For example, declaration 

    Species F(`a) = `a -> `a
is not allowed, since variable `a occurs in a left context.
