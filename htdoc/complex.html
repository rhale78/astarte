LN5. Complex numbers

Species Complex contains complex numbers, represented approximately. You must import math/complex.ast to use complex numbers.

The precision of operations is controlled by the content of box precision!. If precision! holds k, then built-in operations are carried out to at least k decimal digits of precision.

Operations on complex numbers are as follows.

Basic arithmetic operations

+ : (Complex, Complex) -> Complex
x + y is the sum of x and y.

- : (Complex, Complex) -> Complex
x - y is the difference of x and y.

- : Complex -> Complex
-x is the negation of x.

* : (Complex, Complex) -> Complex
x * y is the product of x and y.

/ : (Complex, Complex) -> Complex
x / y is the quotient of x and y.

/ : Complex -> Complex
/z is the reciprocal of z.

^ : (Complex, Natural) -> Complex
z ^ n is z to the power n. Here, z has species Complex and n has species Natural.

^/ : (Complex, Integer) -> Complex
z ^/ i is x to the power i. Here, z has species Complex and i has species Integer.

^~ : (Complex, Complex) -> Complex
x ^~ y is x to the power y. (More precisely it returns the principal value of x to the power y.)

Conversion to string

$ : Complex -> String
$(z) is a string that shows z.

Example: $(complex(2.2,4.0)) = "(2.2+i*4)"

$' : Complex -> String
$'(x) is the decimal representation of x, with commas every three digits from the right.

Example: $(complex(2.2,4.0)) = "(2.2+i*4)"

$$ : (Complex, Natural) -> String
z $$ k is a string that shows complex number z, but uses k characters for each of the real and imaginary parts.

Example: complex(2.2,4.0) $$ 12 = "( 2.2000E+000+ i* 4.0000E+000)"

$$ : (Complex, Natural, Natural) -> String
z $$ (n,k) is the (approximate) decimal representation of z, using at least n total characters, with k characters to the right of the decimal place, for each of the real and imaginary parts.

Example: complex(2.2,4.0) $$ (8,2) = "(     2.20+ i*     4.00)"

$$' : (Complex, Natural, Natural) -> String
x $$' (n,k) is similar to x $$ (n,k), but commas are placed every three digits on the left hand side of the decimal point. The commas count in the total number of characters.

$$ : (Complex, Natural, Natural, Natural) -> String
z $$ (n,k,e) is the (approximate) decimal representation of z, in exponential format, using at least n total characters, with k characters to the right of the decimal place, and using e characters for the exponent, for each of the real and imaginary parts.

Example: 14.1 $$ (12,2,3) = "(    2.20E+001+ i*    4.00E+000)"

Mathematical operations

cos : Complex -> Complex
cos(z) is the (approximate) cosine of z.

exp : Complex -> Complex
exp(z) is (approximately) e to the z.

ln : Complex -> Complex
ln(z) is (approximately) the natural logarithm of z.

log : Complex -> Complex -> Complex
log b z is (approximately) the logarithm to base b of z.

magnitude : Complex -> Real
magnitude(z) is (approximately) the magnitude of z.

sin : Complex -> Complex
sin(z) is the (approximate) sine of z.

sqrt : Complex -> Complex
sqrt(z) is (approximately) the square root of z.

tan : Complex -> Complex
tan(z) is the (approximate) tangent of z.

Miscelleneous operations

complex : (Real, Real) -> Complex
complex(r,s) is complex number with real part r and imaginary part s. If x is of any of the standard numeric species or of species Complex, then complex(x) is the same number converted to species Complex.

fromPolar : (Real, Real) -> Complex
fromPolar(r,theta) is z where z = r*e^(i*theta).

im : Complex -> Real
im(z) is imaginary part of z.

natconst : Natural -> Complex
This definition of natconst allows you to use constants such as 1 as complex numbers.

ratconst : Natural -> Complex
This definition of ratconst allows you to use constants such as 1.0 as complex numbers.

re : Complex -> Real
re(z) is real part of z.

toPolar : Complex -> (Real, Real)
toPolar(z) is (r,theta) where z = r*e^(i*theta).

toSqPolar : Complex -> (Real, Real)
toSqPolar(z) is (r,theta) where z = sqrt(r)*e^(i*theta).

unit : Complex
unit = 1.

zero : Complex
zero = 0.

zero? : Complex -> Boolean
zero?(x) is true if x = 0.
