T12. Parameterization and the Hierarchy

  • The parameterized hierarchy
  • Tying together the genus and community hierarchies
  • Cartesian products and the hierarchy

    • The parameterized hierarchy

    A parameterized genus is similar to a genus. But where a genus is a set of species, a parameterized genus is a set of families. To facilitate talking about parameterized genera, we call them communities (since they are collections of families).

    Communities and families are related to one another in much the same way as genera and species. For example, there is a subcommunity relation among communities.

    Tying together the genus and community hierarchies

    A family or community can belong to a genus. For example, genus ORDER contains species List(T) for every species T that is in genus ORDER. This is done by putting family List in genus ORDER. Lists are ordered lexicographically, with members near the front having higher priority. So, For example, [2,4,8] < [2,5,2].

    There are two kinds of families and communities: opaque and transparent. If you put an opaque family F in a genus G, then G contains F(T) for every species T. If you put a transparent family F in genus G, then G contains F(T) for every species T that is a member of genus G.

    For example, List is a transparent family. Since List is a member of genus ORDER, species List(Natural) is in genus ORDER. But List(Complex) is not in ORDER, since species Complex is not in genus ORDER.

    Cartesian products and the hierarchy

    Cartesian product (ordered pair) species can also belong to genera. Product species are implicitly transparent in both the left and right side. For example, ORDER contains all cartesian product species of the form (S,T), where S and T are both members of ORDER. Ordered pairs are ordered lexicographically, with (a,b) < (c,d) if either a < c or a = c and b < d.
