    • Creating new genera

    Declaration 
           Abstraction REPTILE.
    creates a new genus called REPTILE. Initially, REPTILE has nothing in it.
    Always use a name that starts with an upper case letter for a genus.

    An abstraction declaration can only occur inside an extension.

    Adding species to a genus

    Declaration 
           Relate Lizard isKindOf REPTILE.
    adds species Lizard to genus REPTILE. If SNAKE is a genus, then declaration 
           Relate SNAKE isKindOf REPTILE.
    adds SNAKE (and everything in it) to REPTILE. You can have several things on the right-hand side of isKindOf. For example, 
           Relate SNAKE isKindOf REPTILE, ANIMAL.
    is equivalent to 
           Relate SNAKE isKindOf REPTILE.
       Relate SNAKE isKindOf ANIMAL.

    A relate declaration can only occur inside an extension.

    Relating abstractions when they are created

    When a new abstraction is created, you can indicate that it lies beneath other abstractions. Declaration 
        Abstraction SNAKE isKindOF REPTILE,ANIMAL.
    abbreviates 
        Abstraction SNAKE.
    Relate SNAKE isKindOf REPTILE.
    Relate SNAKE isKindOf ANIMAL.

    Restrictions on extensions of genera

    There are some standard genera that you cannot extend directly.
    ANY
    Genus ANY implicitly contains all species. It is at the top of the genus hierarchy, and all genera are subgenera of ANY. You are not allowed to write 
          Relate T isKindOf ANY.

    RANKED, ENUMERATED
    Genus ENUMERATED is implicitly extended each time you create an enumerated species. You are not allowed to extend ENUMERATED directly. ENUMERATED is a subgenus of RANKED, and you are not allowed to extend RANKED directly either.

    REAL
    Genus REAL and all of it subgenera are fixed, and cannot be extended.

    Specifying meets

    Recall that the meet of two genera G and H is the intersection of G and H, or the largest genus (or species) that is a subgenus or member of both G and H. If the meet of genera G and H is not the empty set, and neither is a subgenus of the other, then you must specify the meet. You do so with a relate declaration. Declaration 
        Relate G & H = J.
    indicates that the meet of G and H is J. For example, suppose that you want to create the following hierarchy, with larger genera to the left. 
                       RHOMBUS
                  /        \
     PARALLELOGRAM          SQUARE
                  \         /
                   RECTANGLE
    The required declarations would be as follows. 
        Extension
      Abstraction PARALLELOGRAM; RHOMBUS; RECTANGLE; SQUARE.
      Relate
        RHOMBUS   isKindOf PARALLELOGRAM;
        RECTANGLE isKindOf PARALLELOGRAM;
        SQUARE    isKindOf RHOMBUS;
        SQUARE    isKindOf RECTANGLE;
        RHOMBUS & RECTANGLE = SQUARE
      %Relate
    %Extension

    Relating genera and families

    A genus can contain a family. For example, genus ORDER contains family List. To add a transparent family or community F to genus G, use 
           Relate F(G) isKindOf G.
    For example, you add List to ORDER by writing 
           Relate List(ORDER) isKindOf ORDER.
    since family List is transparent. You can also write 
           Relate [ORDER] isKindOf ORDER.
    since [ORDER] abbreviates List(ORDER).

    To add an opaque family or community F to genus G, use 

           Relate F(ANY) isKindOf G.

    You can put cartesian product (ordered pair) species in a genus. Product species are implicitly transparent in both the left and right side. If you say 

           Relate (ORDER,ORDER) isKindOf ORDER.
    then you indicate that species (S,T) is a member of genus ORDER, for each S and T in ORDER.

    Meets can be chosen to take into account the nature of the hierarchy. The possible forms of relate declaration for describing meets are as follows. Here, Gx is a genus, Cx a community, S a species and F a family. 

      Relate G1 & G2 = G3
  Relate G1 & G2 = S
  Relate G1 & G2 = F(G)   (F transparent)
  Relate G1 & G2 = F(ANY) (F opaque)
  Relate G & C1 = C2(G)   (C2 transparent)
  Relate G & C1 = C2(ANY) (C2 opaque)
  Relate G & C1 = F(G)    (F transparent)
  Relate G & C1 = F(ANY)  (F opaque)
  Relate C1 & C2 = C3
  Relate C1 & C2 = F
    You can use abbreviations such as [T], and you can write C1 & G rather than G & C1.

    Restrictions on constructing the hierarchy

    There is an important restriction on how the genus hierarchy can be built up. At the end of each extension declaration, the compiler checks meets and joins, and checks that the hierarchy is reasonable. (For example, the meet relation must be associative, so meet(A, meet(B,C)) = meet(meet(A,B), C).) At the next extension, any changes must be consistent with the way things were at the end of the previous extension. You cannot change any meets or joins among genera that were already present.

    Mainly, what this means is that you cannot change the relationships between things except in an exension where one or the other of those things is being created.

    The hierarchy must be designed so that if the meet of A and B is C, then genus C contains exactly those species that are in both A and B. So meets correspond directly to intersections. There is no such requirement, however, of joins. The join of A and B might contain a species that is in neither A nor B.

    Providing descriptions

    You can provide a description of a genus in the declaration that defines it. The description is a string constant, and is put after the name of the genus. For example 
        Abstraction BIRD
      %" Genus BIRD contains various species of birds.
    %Abstraction
