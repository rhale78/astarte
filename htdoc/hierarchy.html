T11. Relations Among Data Abstractions

Data abstractions (genera) are related to one another by the subgenus relation. Generally, genus A is a subgenus of genus B if genus B contains all of the species that A contains, and possibly more. We also say that B is a supergenus of A when A is a subgenus of B.

The subgenus relation gives rise to a hierarchy of genera. For example, here is the hierarchy of standard numeric genera, with species included. Larger genera are to the left. 

                                  Natural
                                /
                         INTEGER
                        /       \
                 RATIONAL         Integer
                /       \       /
            REAL         RATRING
                \       /       \
                 \_RRING        _ Rational
                        \      /
                         RFIELD
                               \
                                \_Real
The hierarchy of Astarte is complete. This means that, given any two genera A and B, you can form the meet, or largest common subgenus (or sometimes common member), of A and B. It is the genus that represents the intersection of A and B, or the largest genus that is a subgenus of both A and B.

For example, the meet of RATIONAL and RRING is RATRING. In some cases, the meet is a species. For example, the meet of RRING and INTEGER is species Integer. In some cases, such as RFIELD and INTEGER, the meet is the empty genus.

There is also a dual notion of the join, or smallest common supergenus, of two genera. The join of A and B is the smallest genus that has both A and B as a subgenus. For example, the join of INTEGER and RFIELD is REAL.
